Graph-spectral backtest

Innovation Momentum: Spectral Dynamics in AI Research

Fourier transforms often replace an awkward coordinate description with a basis in which motion is easier to see. Let us test an analogous move for scientific research fields evolution: transform AI topic histories into graph-spectral modes, measure momentum before 2023, and compare those signals with the growth that followed.

A public AI-topic panel from 1990 through June 2026 is used to ask whether pre-2023 graph Fourier features anticipate 2023 to June 2026 topic growth. The answer is positive but limited: spectral emergence improves on the raw three-year growth baseline in the 2022 holdout, while simple growth remains the most stable signal in rolling backtests. Expanding the count scope from primary-topic labels to broader any-topic assignments increases 2022 assignment coverage by 1.53x and tests whether the conclusion is a measurement artifact.

Main Result

Using only information available through 2022, the spectral emergence score reaches Spearman 0.20 against 2022 to June 2026 topic-share growth. The raw three-year growth baseline reaches 0.18. In this holdout, graph-relative motion contains information that a one-topic trend does not.

The effect is not yet a general forecasting law. Raw 3-year momentum is slightly stronger by Spearman (0.22), while raw one-year acceleration captures more top future growers (4/10 versus 2/10) despite a negative overall rank correlation. In rolling tests from 2000 through 2022, the simple publication-growth baseline remains the most stable feature.

The useful claim is therefore diagnostic: spectral coordinates separate broad field motion, local novelty, and naming shocks in a way that ordinary counts flatten. A stronger claim requires paper-level relations such as citations, co-citation, authorship, and embeddings.

Method: Turn Topic Histories Into Spectral Motion

The physics analogy only matters if it becomes measurable. A topic's position is its normalized attention over time. Its space is a graph linking topics that moved together before the cutoff and share vocabulary. The graph Fourier transform decomposes that motion into smooth, mid-scale, and localized modes.

1. Normalize topic attention

For topic i in year t, convert counts into attention share per million AI works, then apply log scaling and train-window z-scores.

\[ \begin{aligned} s_i(t)&=10^6\frac{c_i(t)}{C_{AI}(t)} \\ x_i(t)&=z_{\mathrm{train}}\!\left(\log(1+s_i(t))\right) \end{aligned} \]

2. Build a pre-cutoff graph

The adjacency matrix combines positive pre-2023 co-movement with metadata similarity, plus conservative within-family edges.

\[ A_{ij}=0.76\,\operatorname{corr}_+\!(x_i,x_j)+0.24\,\operatorname{sim}_{ij} \]

3. Transform into graph Fourier modes

The normalized graph Laplacian supplies the Fourier basis. Low-frequency modes vary smoothly across neighbors; high-frequency modes isolate local deviations.

\[ \begin{aligned} L&=I-D^{-1/2}AD^{-1/2} \\ LU&=U\Lambda \\ \hat{x}(t)&=U^\top x(t) \end{aligned} \]

4. Score momentum and energy

Momentum is recent movement in normalized attention. Spectral energy asks how much yearly signal lives in each graph frequency band.

\[ \begin{aligned} \Delta x_i&=\frac{x_i(2022)-x_i(2019)}{3} \\ E_k(t)&=\hat{x}_k(t)^2 \\ \mathrm{score}&=0.5\,\mathrm{local}+0.3\,\mathrm{midband}+0.2\,\mathrm{acceleration} \end{aligned} \]

After 2022, The Field Looks Less Smooth

In 2022, 97% of spectral energy sits in the low-frequency band. By June 2026, that falls to 69%, while mid- and high-frequency energy rise. In this graph basis, the field moves from broad shared motion toward more localized divergence after the LLM shock.

Spectral energy is descriptive, not causal. It shows that the signal became less smooth in this graph basis; it does not prove why.

Topic labels are too broad to represent "agentic AI" directly, so a phrase-level companion analysis is still useful. Modern LLM-agent vocabulary rises sharply after 2022, while older components such as multi-agent systems, planning, retrieval, dialogue, and tool/code use were already present.

Exact labels are often late. A momentum-space model should track latent capability bundles, not just surface vocabulary.

Summary

Graph Fourier features give a distinct, measurable view of AI topic motion. In the 2022 holdout, spectral emergence is weakly predictive of 2022 to June 2026 topic-share growth and remains close to the best raw momentum feature. Topic size at the cutoff is negatively associated with future growth, so scale alone is not momentum.

The phrase analysis also supports a useful interpretation of agentic AI: the modern label arrives late, while older components such as planning, retrieval, dialogue, and multi-agent systems were already visible. The practical lesson is to track latent capability bundles, not only surface vocabulary.

The stronger test is a paper-level graph built from references, co-citation, authorship, and title/abstract embeddings, with phrase-to-topic bridges for RAG, tool use, LLM agents, chain-of-thought, and planning/action vocabularies. A robustness matrix should compare primary-topic counts, any-topic counts, phrase panels, arXiv categories, and citation-weighted signals before any broad claim about innovation dynamics is made.

Three questions remain open: whether spectral energy transfers precede field mergers or only describe them after the fact; whether graph wavelets localize emerging capability bundles better than global Laplacian modes; and which quantities are stable enough to treat as slow variables in innovation space.
